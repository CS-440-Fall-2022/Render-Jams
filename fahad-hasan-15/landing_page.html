"5 Spheres: A Reflective Odyssey"

Team: "SPECTRE" - 2 members

Theme: Reflection

Image Description:

Corresponding to the set theme, in short, the rendered image visualizes highly reflective objects positioned in close proximity to each other thereby presenting an interesting recursive mirrored effect. To elaborate on the scene itself, let us present the lay of the land. With resepct to the position of the view plane what we see is the front side of the scene - there are in fact a total of five spheres: top (green), bottom (orange), left (red), right(blue) and back (cyan-green). Moreover, there is a plane positioned in such a manner that is slanted downwards - it is the source of the scene's swarth-like white glow. Speaking with respect to the set theme, the image can be interpreted any which way. One can choose to view it literally - the image satisfies the theme so trivially such that, there is no need to discuss the matter further. On the other hand, one could, like this team, interpret the image through the lens of self-reflection. To elaboarte, this scene was birthed out of a metacognitive excercise on our actions at the time and as such, we were embroiled in a ad-infinitum recursive loop of self reflection. Thus, for us this image is best thought of as a mental hall of mirrors. Speaking of the hall of mirrors, that serves material inspiration for the scene itself. Additionally, the lighting and look of the scene finds its inspiration from Stanley Kubrick's 2001: A Space Odyssey and in particular, the so-called, "Stargate" sequence. Well, there is not much else to say then to ask you to watch the film mentioned beforehand and to take a look at our render (click the "5 Spheres: A Reflective Odyssey" heading).

Technical Deatils:

Moving onto the technical details of the scene itself, the scene concerns itself with the notion of indirect illumination in a scene. As such, to be precise, this image is the result of Whitted-style ray-tracing - rather than being simply content with primary rays alone, we take into account secondary rays that is, we are also now tracing reflected rays as they bounce between reflective objects in the scene. As such, The part of the indirect illumination that this models is the specular-to-specular light transport with perfect specular reflection. Now, in order to actually achieve the aforementioned effect, the scene had to be placed/contain reflective materials and surfaces. As such, the main objects of contention, the spheres are dressed with a traditional 'Reflective' material that allows surfaces to be rendered with ambient, diffused and specular direct illumination, as well as reflections. Furthermore, this material has a 'Perfect Specular' BRDF - this allows for perfect mirror reflection to be implemented. For reflection implementation, the aforementioned BRDF is what caters to the compuatation of the direction of the reflected ray, as well as computing the reflected radiance. Having established some of the more setup deatils out of the way, let us explicate more on the reflective phenomenon itself. Now, the spheres are arranged so that their centers are effectively the vertices of tetrahedron - given the 5 spheres the arrangement is effectively that of 2 tetrahedrons with their bases fused together. Additionally, the radii of the spheres are such that they are just about touching (note, none of the spheres overlap with each other). Here, with the precise scene arrangement, the set BRDF parameters - ka (ambient reflection coefficient) = 1.0; kr (reflection coefficient)=1.0; ks (specular reflection coefficient) = 0.5; cr (reflection color) = 1.0; exp (Phong exponent) = 100.0 - and a max_depth value of 200, we emphasize the inter-reflections of the spheres, which are consequently quite bright (and the specular highlight on the spheres are more concentrated). Now, we would ask for one to take a moment and look at the render itself and in particular, focus on the hourglass shaped space that forms between the spheres. We leave this open to interpretation but to us, the pattern formed very closely resembles the Sierpinski Gasket, a mathematical fractal. While we cannot fully explain the patterned form what we can do is posit that it has something to do with the similar recursive structures - the gasket being the subdivision of triangles a set number of times while the scene itself is just reflections of spheres. As for additional remaining technical details, the scene is lit by three sources of light: (i) ambient light at 0.25 luminance (ii) Point light in the top-left corner of the scene at 3.0 luminance (iii) Directional light in the top-left corner of the scene at 2.0 luminance. Furthermore, a whitted tracer is used given that the scene is concerned with perfect specular reflection.
